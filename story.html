Our Story

78 Years of the Mile

From postwar Phoenix to today's revival — the story of Arizona's first shopping district outside downtown.

In 1946, downtown Phoenix business owners looked east along McDowell Road and saw opportunity. What they built became the Valley's first major commercial district outside the city center — and the first in Phoenix listed on the National Register of Historic Places.

For nearly two decades, the Miracle Mile thrived as an automobile-friendly shopping strip. Families drove in from across the Valley. At its peak in the mid-1950s, more than 50 shops lined the road. Then the malls came, and the Mile went quiet.

But it never disappeared. The businesses that stayed kept the lights on through decades of decline. And starting around 2014, a new generation of entrepreneurs — many of them immigrants, many millennials, 60% minority-owned — began filling the empty storefronts with something new.

Today, the Miracle Mile is back. Not as a copy of what it was, but as something better: a diverse, authentic, locally-rooted corridor that reflects what Phoenix actually looks like in 2026.

The Timeline

1946

The Beginning

Downtown Phoenix business owners expand east along McDowell Road, creating the Valley's first major shopping district outside the city center.

1954

The Name

The stretch from 16th to 24th Street on McDowell earns the nickname "Miracle Mile" — a thriving, auto-friendly commercial strip at the heart of a booming postwar Phoenix.

1958

The Shift

Park Central mall opens — Arizona's first. Suburban shopping pulls customers away. The Mile begins a long decline as McDowell becomes a pass-through road.

1991

The Arch

Artist Michelle Stuhl's 85-foot Gateway Arch is installed over McDowell Road — a steel and white enamel landmark that becomes the symbol of the Mile's enduring identity.

2014

The Association

The Miracle Mile Merchants Association formalizes, uniting neighbors, business owners, and stakeholders to champion the corridor's revival.

2016

The Investment

LISC MetroEdge completes a corridor assessment. Millions in grants, forgivable loans, and low-interest loans flow into the district. Murals, block parties, and cleanups transform the streetscape.

2019

Miracle Mile Days Returns

The original 1950s community celebration is revived on November 16th, marking a turning point in the Mile's identity as a destination again.

2025

Arizona Wilderness Arrives

Arizona Wilderness Brewing Co. opens its 3rd location on the Mile, shifting all beer production to McDowell Road. A major anchor for the corridor's future.

Now

The Revival

80+ businesses. Low vacancy. National Register of Historic Places listing. 60% minority-owned. The Mile is back — and it's better than ever.

The Landmark

The Gateway Arch

Commissioned in 1990 and installed in 1991, the Gateway Arch was created by artist Michelle Stuhl. At 85 feet long, the white enamel steel structure spans McDowell Road and has become the defining symbol of the Miracle Mile.

Its design features Roman numeral-like elements and a picket fence motif. The white enamel paint catches light differently throughout the day — golden at sunrise, brilliant white at noon, warm amber at sunset.

National Register of Historic Places

The Miracle Mile is the first commercial district in Phoenix to be listed on the National Register of Historic Places, recognizing its significance as a pioneering auto-oriented commercial strip that defined postwar suburban development in Arizona.
